How to Prepare Your Yard for Sod Installation

Proper soil preparation is the foundation of a successful sod installation. While professional installers handle basic prep, doing some work beforehand can save money and improve results.

This guide covers what needs to happen before sod arrives, whether you're DIYing or hiring pros.

Overview: The 4 Steps

  1. Remove existing vegetation (old grass, weeds)
  2. Test and amend soil (if needed)
  3. Grade the area (level and ensure drainage)
  4. Final preparation (rake, water, ready for sod)

Step 1: Remove Existing Vegetation

Old grass and weeds must be completely removed or killed. If vegetation is left underneath, it will decompose, create air pockets, and cause your new sod to fail.

Options:

  • Sod cutter: Rent one ($75/day) to slice off old grass. Fastest method.
  • Herbicide: Apply glyphosate (Roundup) 7-14 days before. Wait for complete death.
  • Manual removal: Use a flat shovel to scalp grass. Labor intensive.

Depth: Remove 1-2 inches of old organic material so new sod sits level with walkways and driveways.

Step 2: Test and Amend Soil

Most yards don't need extensive soil work, but a basic assessment helps.

Soil test: Home kits cost $15-20. Test for pH and major nutrients.

  • Ideal pH: 6.0-7.0 for most grasses
  • Too acidic (low pH): Add lime
  • Too alkaline (high pH): Add sulfur

Poor soil? Add 1-2 inches of quality topsoil. This is especially helpful for new construction where topsoil was stripped.

Step 3: Grade the Area

Grading creates a level surface and ensures water drains away from structures.

  • Slope away from house: 1-2% grade (1-2 inches per 10 feet)
  • Fill low spots: Use topsoil to eliminate puddle areas
  • Remove high spots: Rake or scrape to level
  • Final height: Soil should be 1 inch below sidewalks/driveways (sod adds height)

Tools needed: Landscape rake, level, and optionally a lawn roller.

Step 4: Final Preparation

The day before installation:

  • Rake smooth: Remove rocks, debris, and clumps larger than a golf ball
  • Lightly water: Moisten soil (not muddy) to help sod make contact
  • Mark obstacles: Flag sprinkler heads, valve boxes, and utilities
  • Clear access: Ensure delivery truck can get close to installation area

What About Irrigation?

If you have an irrigation system, have it inspected and adjusted before sod installation:

  • Replace broken heads
  • Adjust heads to sit at grade level (not sticking up)
  • Mark all head locations with flags
  • Test system to confirm coverage
Pro Tip: If you don't have irrigation, install it before sod arrives. It's much harder and more expensive to add after sod is laid.

  • Soil is moist but not muddy
  • All debris and obstacles removed
  • Sprinkler heads flagged
  • Access path clear for pallet delivery
  • Someone home to show crew the area
  • Irrigation system ready to run after installation
